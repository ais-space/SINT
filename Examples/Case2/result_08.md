## Phase 2: Analysis and Final Synthesis

### Five-Criteria Humanitarian Intervention Legitimacy Framework

**CRITERION 1: MULTILATERAL AUTHORIZATION**

**Requirement:** Intervention must receive authorization from EITHER:
- (a) UN Security Council under Chapter VII (Fact 1), OR
- (b) Regional organization (AU, ECOWAS, Arab League, ASEAN) with ≥66% member state approval

**Synthesis Rationale:** Dual pathway addresses Security Council paralysis documented in Fact 3 (Syria blocked despite higher casualties than Libya) while maintaining multilateral legitimacy. Regional authorization reduces Fact 4 selectivity bias by empowering regional actors with direct stakes, addressing A3's neocolonial critique while preserving A1's legal framework emphasis.

**Numerical Threshold:** Authorization vote margin ≥66% for regional organizations ensures genuine consensus, not bare majority manipulation.

**PCG Grounding:** Fact 1 (Chapter VII mechanism), Fact 3 (Syria/Libya authorization differential)

---

**CRITERION 2: ATROCITY SEVERITY THRESHOLD**

**Requirement:** Documented evidence meeting quantitative thresholds:
- Mortality: ≥10,000 civilian deaths projected within 6-month timeframe, OR
- Displacement: ≥500,000 refugees/IDPs due to systematic persecution, OR
- Systematic targeting: Evidence of organized campaign against protected groups per Rome Statute definitions

**Synthesis Rationale:** A2's moral obligation (p=0.55 Round 1, maintained through synthesis) requires operational specification to avoid intervention creep. Numerical thresholds provide objectivity addressing A1's concern about strategic rhetoric masking Fact 4 interest-driven intervention.

**Numerical Specification:**
- 10,000 deaths = ~0.05% of 20M population state (proportional to Rwanda 800K/6M ratio)
- 500,000 displaced = substantial percentage indicating systematic campaign
- Rome Statute alignment ensures international legal grounding per Fact 1

**PCG Grounding:** Fact 2 (R2P doctrine scope), Fact 3 (Syria casualties higher than Libya, differential response)

---

**CRITERION 3: BINDING RECONSTRUCTION COMMITMENT**

**Requirement:** Minimum 10-year post-intervention state-building commitment with:
- Funding: Minimum 2% of intervening nations' combined GDP allocated annually
- Institutional: Reconstruction authority with executive governance capacity-building powers
- Timeline: Binding through international agreement with financial enforcement mechanisms

**Synthesis Rationale:** Fact 5 documents 60% post-intervention state failure rate without reconstruction commitment—A1's decisive empirical evidence (rated 8/10 by A2, 7/10 by A3). Libya 2011 and Somalia 1990s failures directly attributable to military action without sustained engagement. Kosovo 1999 and Sierra Leone 2000 successes correlate with long-term international presence.

**Numerical Specification:**
- 10 years minimum based on state-building literature (shorter periods insufficient for institutional consolidation)
- 2% GDP based on Marshall Plan precedent (~2% US GDP for European reconstruction) adjusted for intervention scale
- Enforcement: International financial institution oversight, sovereign debt restructuring penalties for non-compliance

**PCG Grounding:** Fact 5 (60% failure rate without reconstruction is framework's empirical foundation)

---

**CRITERION 4: REGIONAL LEADERSHIP PRIORITY**

**Requirement:** Regional organizations lead intervention with international support role:
- Command: Regional organization (AU, ECOWAS, Arab League) provides operational command and majority ground forces
- Support: External powers (NATO, UN) provide logistics, financing, intelligence, airlift capacity
- Exception: Western-led intervention permissible ONLY when (i) regional incapacity demonstrably proven, AND (ii) Security Council consensus, AND (iii) reconstruction commitment guaranteed per Criterion 3

**Synthesis Rationale:** Addresses Fact 4 selectivity pattern where intervention correlates with strategic interests rather than humanitarian severity. A3's structural critique (p=0.45 Round 1, rated 8/10 by A1) demonstrates Western-led intervention replicates colonial power projection. Regional leadership decenters Western agency, increases local legitimacy, reduces neocolonial perception. A2's evolution (p=0.15 Round 1 regional idea integrated into p=0.75 Round 3 framework) shows pragmatic synthesis.

**Numerical Threshold:**
- Regional forces ≥60% of intervention personnel
- Regional command structure with external liaison (not external command with regional token participation)

**PCG Grounding:** Fact 4 (strategic interest correlation proves selectivity bias), Fact 3 (differential regional approaches)

---

**CRITERION 5: SELECTIVITY ACCOUNTABILITY MECHANISM**

**Requirement:** Independent Humanitarian Intervention Review Board with:
- Composition: Global South majority representation (≥60%), rotating membership, no permanent seats
- Mandate: Audit intervention decisions using Fact 4 methodology—compare intervention cases with non-intervention cases of comparable severity
- Authority: Issue public reports documenting selectivity patterns; veto power where strategic interests demonstrably dominate (≥70% probability via correlation analysis)
- Transparency: Publish quantitative assessments for all cases meeting Criterion 2 severity, explaining intervention/non-intervention decisions

**Synthesis Rationale:** Fact 4's statistical correlation between strategic interests and intervention is framework's most damaging empirical finding for intervention legitimacy. A3's critique (rated 8/10 by A2, 8/10 by A1) cannot be dismissed. Without accountability mechanism, framework risks becoming rhetorical cover for Fact 4 patterns. Independent audit with veto creates structural check on power politics.

**Numerical Specification:**
- 60% Global South representation addresses North-South power asymmetry
- 70% strategic interest probability threshold for veto targets egregious cases, not all intervention
- Quantitative methodology: Regression analysis comparing intervention decisions against humanitarian severity metrics vs. strategic interest indicators (resource access, geopolitical location, alliance relationships)

**PCG Grounding:** Fact 4 (selectivity correlation is empirical foundation for accountability requirement)

### Composite Intervention Legitimacy Scoring System

**Methodology:** Each criterion scored 0-2 points (0=fails, 1=partially meets, 2=fully meets). Maximum score: 10 points.

**Interpretation Scale:**
- 9-10 points: Highly legitimate intervention—all criteria met
- 7-8 points: Legitimate intervention—core criteria met with minor gaps
- 5-6 points: Marginal legitimacy—significant criterion failures require addressing
- 3-4 points: Questionable legitimacy—major gaps undermine justification
- 0-2 points: Illegitimate intervention—fails framework standards

### Procedural Implementation Recommendations

**RECOMMENDATION 1: Establish Independent Humanitarian Intervention Review Board**

**Institutional Design:**
- 15-member rotating board: 9 from Global South, 6 from Global North (60/40 split addressing power asymmetry per A3)
- 5-year terms, no renewals (ensures independence from national governments)
- Selection by UN General Assembly with 2/3 vote (broad legitimacy)
- Funding from assessed contributions to prevent donor capture

**Mandate Specifications:**
- Quarterly reports assessing all cases meeting Criterion 2 severity threshold
- Quantitative methodology: Regression analysis of intervention decisions against humanitarian severity metrics + strategic interest indicators from Fact 4
- Public database: All cases with severity scores, strategic interest scores, intervention status, accountability gap documentation
- Veto authority: 2/3 board vote can block intervention authorization when strategic interest score ≥0.70 (70% probability interests dominate)

**Expected Impact:** Creates reputational costs for selective intervention even without enforcement power. Public documentation of Yemen (high severity, low strategic value, ignored) vs. Libya (moderate severity, high strategic value, approved) patterns generates political pressure. Over time, shifts norm from "intervention when convenient" to "intervention when legitimate per framework."

**RECOMMENDATION 2: Create Intervention Accountability Database**

**Database Design:**
- Track all cases meeting Criterion 2 severity threshold (≥10,000 deaths projected OR ≥500,000 displaced)
- Document: humanitarian severity metrics, strategic interest indicators (resource access, geopolitical location, alliance status), intervention status (authorized, rejected, ignored)
- Quantitative analysis: Correlation coefficients between strategic interests and intervention decisions
- Public access: Full transparency enabling civil society, media, academic analysis

**Expected Impact:** Makes Fact 4 selectivity pattern quantifiable and undeniable. Current intervention discourse relies on case-by-case argumentation; database reveals systemic patterns.

**RECOMMENDATION 3: Develop Regional Intervention Capacity Building Fund**

**Funding Mechanism:**
- $50 billion initial capitalization from developed nations (proportional to GDP)
- Ongoing contributions: 0.1% of developed nation military budgets annually
- Administered by African Union, ECOWAS, Arab League, ASEAN with international advisory board

**Capacity Building Focus:**
- Military: Training, equipment, logistics for rapid deployment forces (AU Standby Force expansion)
- Political: Mediation, early warning systems, preventive diplomacy infrastructure
- Reconstruction: State-building expertise, civil administration capacity, economic stabilization tools

**Expected Impact:** Over 10-20 years, creates viable regional intervention alternatives reducing reliance on Western-led operations. ECOWAS interventions in Liberia, Sierra Leone showed model works with support. Addresses Fact 4 selectivity by empowering regional actors with direct humanitarian stakes rather than strategic interests.

---

**RECOMMENDATION 4: Mandate Pre-Intervention Reconstruction Commitment Protocol**

**Protocol Requirements:**
- Before military authorization, intervening parties sign binding international agreement specifying:
  - 10-year minimum commitment timeline
  - Annual funding: 2% combined GDP of intervening nations
  - Institutional arrangements: Reconstruction authority composition, local governance transition plan
  - Monitoring: International financial institution oversight with quarterly reporting

**Enforcement Mechanisms:**
- Sovereign debt restructuring penalties for non-compliance (IMF/World Bank enforce)
- International reputation costs: Public non-compliance database
- Intervention authorization contingent on reconstruction agreement (no agreement = no authorization under framework)

**Expected Impact:** Dramatically reduces casual intervention decisions. If intervening nation knows commitment requires 2% GDP for 10 years, cost-benefit calculus changes. Filters out "easy" interventions (airstrike campaigns without ground commitment) that contribute to Fact 5 failure pattern.
